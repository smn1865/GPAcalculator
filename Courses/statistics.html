Descriptive Statistics

Fundamental tools of descriptive statistics — data collection, grouping, visualisation and summary indicators — to describe datasets and support data-driven conclusions in science and applied fields.

3 ECTS 21 in-class hours 60 hours individual work

Credits & Workload

The course carries 3 ECTS credits and includes a total of 21 contact hours, delivered as lectures combined with practical work. There is no separate laboratory or project component specified. In addition, students are expected to complete approximately 60 hours of individual work over the semester.

Aim of the Course

The aim of “Descriptive Statistics” is to introduce students to the main tools of descriptive statistics and to develop skills in collecting, organising, visualising and summarising data. The course supports the formation of statistical thinking and the initial ability to draw conclusions from data using descriptive methods.

Learning Outcomes - Knowledge

By the end of the course, students are expected to:

  • Describe the principles for solving problems in natural and applied sciences using statistical models.
  • Present collected data effectively using tables, graphs and summary descriptive measures.
  • Recognise and distinguish types of variables, in particular qualitative and quantitative characteristics.

Learning Outcomes - Application of Professional Knowledge

  • Use effective tools and modern software environments for statistical data processing.
  • Group data according to research objectives and produce appropriate graphical representations.
  • Compute and interpret basic statistical indicators such as means, measures of central tendency and dispersion.

Learning Outcomes - Transferable Skills

  • Use professional statistical and methodological literature.
  • Present statistical results clearly and precisely in both oral and written form.
  • Model real-world problems and solve them by applying suitable statistical methods.
  • Work effectively with digital data processing tools and spreadsheets when handling datasets.

Assessment & Grading

Assessment is based on written examinations, including an intermediate test and a final exam. All evaluations are individual. The intermediate exam usually combines a multiple-choice test with commentary and 3–4 typical problems with sub-questions. The final exam consists of 4–5 typical problems with several sub-questions each.

The final grade is composed of two written assessments:

  • Short written test - 1 h 10 min, weight: 40%.
  • Final written exam - 2 h, weight: 60%.

Each correct intermediate step is graded, even if the final numerical answer is not correct; such steps receive from 0.25 points upwards depending on importance. Copying only the problem statement or solving a different problem than the assigned one results in 0 points for that task.

Numerical errors typically result in a deduction of 0.25–0.5 points from the points allocated to the problem; if a numerical mistake does not affect the logic of the solution, no more than 0.5 points are subtracted. Logical errors lead to at least 0.5 points being deducted, depending on severity.

Teaching Methods & Prerequisites

Teaching is practice-oriented and based on real and realistic examples, with systematic solution of diverse types of problems. Lectures are closely linked with practical work so that theoretical concepts are immediately applied to data.

To enrol in “Descriptive Statistics”, students should have school-level knowledge of algebra. This prerequisite ensures familiarity with basic numerical operations and formulas used in statistical calculations.

Course Content

Topic 1 - Grouping Statistical Data (1.5 hours)

Grouping of statistical data; absolute and relative frequencies; tabular and graphical representation of data distributions. Construction and interpretation of basic charts (histograms, bar charts, pie charts).

Topic 2 - Measures of Central Tendency (3 hours)

Computation and interpretation of different mean values:

  • Arithmetic mean
  • Geometric mean
  • Harmonic mean
  • Quadratic (root mean square) mean

Discussion of the contexts in which each mean is appropriate.

Topic 3 - Positional Measures (3 hours)

Descriptive characteristics based on position in the distribution:

  • Mode
  • Median
  • Quartiles
  • Deciles

Use of these indicators to describe the centre and spread of data.

Topic 4 - Measures of Dispersion (3 hours)

Variation indicators that capture the spread of data:

  • Range
  • Mean absolute deviation
  • Variance
  • Standard deviation
  • Coefficient of variation

Interpretation of dispersion measures and comparison of variability between datasets.

Topic 5 - Measures of Concentration (3 hours)

Concentration indicators and inequality in distributions:

  • Concentration coefficient
  • Lorenz curve
  • Gini coefficient

Applications to income and wealth distributions and other concentrated phenomena.

Topic 6 - Contingency Tables for Two Variables (3 hours)

Two-way contingency tables; calculation and interpretation of overall and within-group descriptive indicators for two variables; analysis of association between categorical or grouped quantitative variables.

Topic 7 - Simple Linear Regression (3 hours)

Simple linear regression model; estimation of the coefficients of the regression line; computation and interpretation of the linear correlation coefficient; assessing the strength and direction of linear relationships.

Topic 8 - Nonlinear Regression & Forecasting (1.5 hours)

Construction of regression models of parabolic, hyperbolic, exponential, power and logarithmic forms using computer tools. Performing forecasts based on fitted models and discussing limitations of extrapolation.
